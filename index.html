Space Liner 77

A Cozy Chaos Spaceship Simulator (In Development)

You are a slightly overwhelmed spaceship captain transporting autonomous, quirky passengers between spaceports. You don't control the passengers—you manage the failing ship systems, cook space fricassee, and desperately try to prevent systemic panic.

Core Features:

  • Systemic Chaos: Keep up with broken bunk beds, dirty space-guacamole spots, and failing power generators.
  • Autonomous Moods: Passengers move, eat, and send grumpy social media updates based on their personal needs.
  • Spaceship upgrades: Earn coins from satisfied passengers and improve your ship at the spaceport. You can also hire services to handle unfinished tasks, like patching those holes a pet martian possum did on the couch while you were busy putting out a fire in the kitchen.
  • No Violence, Pure Frustration: Light corporate satire and wholesome, comedic panic.
"I can’t control everything… but I can keep things from collapsing."

About

Rafael Cortez Sanchez (RAFAISKA)

I am a Backend Software Engineer with more than six years of experience building reliable backend systems, REST APIs, and distributed applications. By day, I work on billing systems and cloud infrastructure in Linux-based environments. By night, I apply those same engineering principles to indie game development.

My academic research focused on Neural Architecture Search, and early in my career, I built complex traffic control simulations. Because of that background, I don't see games as just graphics—I see them as real-time simulation engines.

I build my games with the Godot Engine, leveraging its modular, object-oriented design to create robust state machines and autonomous agent behaviors. When I am not optimizing data pipelines, I am figuring out how to stop simulated alien passengers from rioting over space burgers because of a bottleneck in a spaceship's resource queue.

Besides coding, I also make music and pixel art for my games.

Music

I started taking piano lessons when I was 14, and as soon as I could read my first sheet music, I also started putting together MIDI files with Anvil Studio. These days, I prefer using trackers such as MilkyTracker to make music inspired by the classic Game Boy. Trackers make that setup easier: just 4 channels (3 square waves + 1 noise), minimal samples, and simple effects.

Pixel art

Unlike music, I didn't study art for very long. I took some drawing classes a long time ago, and there are still a few pieces from that time on my long-forgotten DeviantArt profile. I really enjoy drawing by hand, but there is never enough time to practice it.

I used to love drawing when I was a kid, so when I first discovered MS Paint on my father's i486 PC, I also became interested in creating low-resolution digital art. I rediscovered this passion recently, when I was forced to make art for my games. Pixel art seemed like an easier option for someone without formal education in art fundamentals, but I was surprised by how hard it is to express yourself with just a small bunch of pixels and colors. I've been learning a lot since then.

My weapon of choice for pixel art is Aseprite.
